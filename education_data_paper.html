Abstract

We present the Lokentra U.S. Email Security Index (ESI), a comprehensive multi-sector entity registry with DNS email security profiling covering 406,680 domains across the United States. This paper describes the education subset: 22,741 K-12 school districts and charter LEAs and 3,246 higher education institutions spanning all 50 states and the District of Columbia, sourced from the NCES Common Core of Data (CCD) 2024-2025 school year, IPEDS, and state education agency directories. Each entity is linked to its internet domain, email provider, and a full DNS authentication profile (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) scored on a 100-point rubric. We find that K-12 DMARC adoption stands at 51.7%, compared to 86.1% for higher education — a 34.4 percentage-point gap attributable to institutional IT capacity, vendor ecosystem effects, and the absence of federal email security mandates for sub-federal entities. Google Workspace dominates K-12 email infrastructure at a 4:1 ratio over Microsoft 365, while higher education inverts this pattern. Only 8.2% of K-12 districts deploy email security gateways, versus 20.4% for higher education. The dataset includes phone numbers, physical addresses, grade spans, NCES LEAIDs, and operational school counts for 19,281 matched entities, enabling direct linkage to NCES enrollment, finance, and demographic datasets. To our knowledge, this is the largest entity-attributed email security dataset for the U.S. education sector.

Keywords: email security, DMARC, SPF, K-12, higher education, DNS analysis, public sector cybersecurity, Google Workspace, education technology

1. Introduction

Email remains the primary attack vector against educational institutions. Phishing campaigns targeting school districts have increased substantially, with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) reporting education as the most-targeted sector for ransomware incidents in 2023-2024. Despite growing federal attention to government cybersecurity, Binding Operational Directive (BOD) 18-01 — which mandated DMARC at enforcement level for federal agencies — does not extend to the approximately 130,000 K-12 schools and 6,000+ postsecondary institutions operating across the United States.

The security posture of these education entities is largely unmeasured. Existing studies focus on federal .gov domains or narrow state-level samples. No prior work has attempted a comprehensive, entity-attributed DNS analysis covering all U.S. K-12 districts and accredited institutions with email provider classification, security gateway detection, and authentication scoring.

This paper makes three contributions:

  1. A nationwide education entity registry linking 25,987 education entities to their internet domains, sourced from NCES CCD (2024-2025), IPEDS, and state education agency directories.
  2. Full-spectrum DNS profiling of education domains across seven record types, with email provider classification, gateway detection, and a 100-point authentication score.
  3. Empirical findings on the K-12/higher-ed security divide, the Google-Microsoft bifurcation, and the gateway adoption gap — with implications for federal cybersecurity policy.

The CCD 2024-2025 file (ccd_lea_029_2425_w_1a_073025.csv) provides 19,630 LEA records, of which 19,281 have SY_STATUS=1 (Open). These were ingested via a deterministic pipeline that matches existing entities by NCES LEAID and inserts unmatched entities as new records. The CCD file provides phone numbers, physical and mailing addresses, grade span, operational school count, charter status, and LEA type classification for 100% of open LEAs.

3.2 DNS Profiling Pipeline

Domain extraction isolates the apex domain from each entity's website URL. DNS resolution proceeds in seven stages:

  1. MX resolution — 50-thread parallel resolution via dnspython
  2. Provider classification — MX hostname pattern matching (Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Barracuda, Proofpoint, etc.)
  3. SPF/DMARC/DKIM resolution — TXT record parsing with qualifier extraction
  4. Gateway detection — MX vs. SPF divergence analysis to identify email security proxies
  5. Underlying provider inference — SPF include: analysis to detect the real mailbox platform behind gateways
  6. Scoring — 100-point rubric applied to each domain
  7. Deduplication — SHA-1 keyed on normalized_name | entity_type | county

K-12 districts overwhelmingly use Google Workspace, consistent with Google Workspace for Education's free or heavily discounted licensing for K-12. Higher education institutions invert this pattern, favoring Microsoft 365 at a 2.9:1 ratio. This is the first empirical quantification of this bifurcation at national scale.

48.3% of K-12 domains have no DMARC record. These districts are fully vulnerable to domain spoofing — an attacker can send email appearing to come from superintendent@district.org and it will be delivered to recipients' inboxes without authentication failure. Higher education achieves 86.1% DMARC adoption, a 34.4 percentage-point lead likely attributable to larger IT teams, dedicated security staff, and higher cybersecurity awareness.

6. Research Applications

6.1 Education Policy

  • Cybersecurity readiness assessment — identify the most vulnerable states and districts
  • Federal mandate gap analysis — model the impact of extending BOD 18-01 to K-12
  • Digital equity — correlate email security with district funding, rurality, and demographics via NCES LEAID linkage
  • State policy effectiveness — compare states with and without cybersecurity mandates

6.2 EdTech and Procurement

  • Cloud adoption patterns — quantify Google vs. Microsoft market share by segment and state
  • Vendor lock-in — analyze switching barriers created by free Google Workspace for Education
  • Email security gateway TAM — 8,200+ K-12 districts without gateways represent an addressable market

6.3 Cybersecurity Research

  • Phishing risk modeling — map authentication gaps to phishing exposure at the district level
  • DMARC deployment barriers — investigate why 48% of K-12 lacks DMARC despite having SPF
  • Longitudinal tracking — use this dataset as a baseline for measuring future adoption

8. Limitations

  • Website coverage. 3,467 K-12 entities (15.2%) lack a website URL, precluding DNS analysis. These tend to be small charter LEAs or specialized agencies.
  • County field sparsity. The CCD LEA file does not include county; county attribution for newly inserted entities requires secondary enrichment via NCES LEAID.
  • DNS temporal validity. DNS records are subject to change. The analysis was conducted at a point in time (March 2026) and does not reflect subsequent migrations.
  • DKIM selector enumeration. Only common Google Workspace and Microsoft 365 selectors are checked. Entities using non-standard selectors may be underscored.
  • Gateway classification. 39.8% of proxied domains have "Unknown" underlying provider, meaning true Google/M365 adoption is higher than MX-visible counts.
